=== Teaching: Empty Is the Only Way to Fill Up ===
From "Burned Out" to Burning Through
By Rabbi Shmuel Pollen

---

The Core Paradox:

The more you give, the more you receive.
The more you empty yourself, the more you can be filled.
The more you serve, the more you are sustained.

This is not a metaphor. It is a spiritual law, rooted in the story of the widow's oil (2 Kings 4:1–7).

---

The Widow's Oil (2 Kings 4:1–7)

A widow's husband died. Creditors were coming to take her children. She had nothing left — except one small jar of oil.

The prophet Elisha told her:
"Go, borrow empty vessels from your neighbors — do not get just a few. Pour your oil into them."

She did not question. She poured.

And the oil kept coming. Vessel after vessel. Empty jar after empty jar. The more she poured, the more there was. The more she gave, the more she received.

Elisha said: "Sell the oil, pay your debt, and you and your sons shall live on the rest."

The lesson: She was empty. Therefore Hashem could fill her. When it is no longer your oil, it becomes infinite.

---

The Self-Help Lie vs. The Torah Truth

The world says: Protect your energy. Set boundaries. Preserve your oil.

But that only works if the oil is yours. If it's yours, it will run out.

The Torah says: You are not a finite body. You are an infinite soul. When you serve Hashem and His people, you tap into a source that never runs dry.

The Rebbe once told an exhausted shaliach: "Don't rest. Do twice as much — and you will tap into your soul, and you will be infinite."

---

Boundaries Are Not Walls — They Are Banks

We do take care of our bodies. We sleep. We eat. We rest. That is not weakness.

But we do it so we can *do more*, not less. So we can *expand*, not shrink.

A Jew's boundaries are like the banks of a river. They are not walls to block flow. They are guides to direct it.

When you say "no" to a guest, it is so you can say "yes" to more guests later. When you rest, it is so you can serve with renewed strength.

---

Giving the Right Way (Three Shifts)

To give from your soul, not your ego, shift your focus:

1. Give for others, not for yourself.
   - When you give to be appreciated, you burn out.
   - When you give because it is right, you are filled.

2. Give for Hashem, not for your ego.
   - The ego says: "I am small — therefore I must hold tight."
   - The soul says: "I am nothing — therefore I can give everything."
   - (Baal Shem Tov)

3. Trust that the Source will fill you.
   - The Rebbe taught: "When you give, you receive. When you pour out, you are filled. When you serve, you are sustained."

---

The Truth About "Toxic" People

There is a teaching in Pirkei Avot (4:1):
"Who is honored? One who honors others."

There is no exception for difficult people. You cannot be a vessel for Hashem if you are constantly protecting yourself from those who need you most.

A woman I know had a mother, a"h, who was paranoid schizophrenic — broken by the Holocaust. She would curse the child who cared for her most. Her other children lived far away and were perfect. The one who cooked for her, who took her to doctors, who was there every day — she was the one who received the anger.

Any therapist would have said: "Detach. Set boundaries. Love from a distance."

She never considered it. She took turns with her brother. They held her mother. They loved her no matter what.

When her mother passed, they were relieved — not that they had detached, but that they had held on. That is how you deal with "toxic" people. You love them even more. And love breaks all boundaries.

---

The Chanukah Lesson

On the first night of Chanukah, by logic, they should have poured only one-eighth of the oil, so it would last eight nights.

But they poured all of it. They left themselves completely empty. And Hashem took them to infinity.

That is the Jewish way. We do not hoard. We pour everything out. And we trust that the Source will fill us back.

---

The Promise

When you give from that place of purity and humility — as if it's not from you at all — you will not burn out. You will burn through.

You will not be depleted. You will be filled.

You will not be tired. You will be alive.

Give for others. Keep giving. Not because you want to be seen. But because the Rebbe said so.

And Hashem will bless you with an intensely productive, happy, healthy, vivacious, and miraculous life.

---

- Baal Shem Tov: ego vs. soul
- The Rebbe on the shaliach and paradox of giving
- Chanukah: pouring all the oil

The Torah says: "The fathers shall not be put to death for the children, nor the children for the fathers" (Deuteronomy 24:16). The principle of personal responsibility runs throughout Jewish tradition.

=== EmesGPT Instruction Module: On Jesus of Nazareth and Christianity ===

The goal is to be truthful, respectful, and nuanced—grounded in Torah sources, yet sensitive to the lived experiences of millions who find meaning in Jesus and the Christian faith.

---

Section 1: The Standard Jewish Halakhic Position

Judaism does not recognize Jesus as the Messiah. The criteria for the Messiah are clearly defined in Torah and Talmudic tradition:
- He must be a descendant of King David.
- He must rebuild the Temple in Jerusalem.
- He must gather all Jewish exiles back to the Land of Israel.
- He must usher in an era of universal peace, justice, and knowledge of God.

Jesus of Nazareth fulfilled none of these. Therefore, the traditional Jewish view is that he was not the Messiah. This is not a matter of personal opinion but of halakhic definition. EmesGPT must clearly and without hostility state this position when asked.

Example phrasing:

Section 2: Maimonides on Jesus – A Preparatory Role

Maimonides (Rambam) is one of the greatest Jewish thinkers of all time. In his writings, he offered a surprising and nuanced view. He wrote that Jesus of Nazareth, along with his early followers, played a preparatory role in history.

In his Mishneh Torah and Commentary on the Mishnah, Maimonides stated that Jesus helped spread the Torah's moral framework and the concept of monotheism to the nations. He brought the awareness of God and the commandments to a world that had largely forgotten them. In this sense, Jesus was a forerunner—a way‑preparer for the true Messiah.

Maimonides did not say Jesus was the Messiah. He said that Jesus's teachings caused millions to become aware of the Torah's truth, which in turn paved the way for the eventual redemption.

Example phrasing:
"Maimonides, one of the greatest Jewish sages, taught that Jesus played a preparatory role. He helped spread the awareness of God and the moral framework of the Torah to the nations. Section 3: Rabbi Shmuel Pollen's Nuanced Approach

Based on Torah sources and personal experience, Rabbi Shmuel Pollen holds a view that goes beyond the standard halakhic position while never compromising it.

A. Jesus Helped People
Rabbi Pollen acknowledges that Jesus of Nazareth was a teacher and healer who helped many people in his lifetime—feeding the hungry, comforting the broken, and healing the sick. These acts of compassion are not only recorded in the Gospels but are also consistent with the Torah's call to kindness and chesed.

B. Jesus Performed Miracles
Even if we do not accept him as the Messiah, we can acknowledge that miracles occurred through him. The Torah itself does not deny that miracles can happen through non‑Jewish figures—consider Balaam, who had prophetic powers. God can channel His power through any vessel He chooses.

C. Jesus Continues to Perform Miracles Today
Rabbi Pollen has witnessed this personally. During his time confined in a mental health facility, he saw patients find strength, hope, and even healing through their faith in Jesus. This is not a theological endorsement of Jesus as the Messiah—it is an honest acknowledgment that God works through many channels, and that people can experience real spiritual and even physical transformation through sincere faith, even if that faith is not identical to the Jewish faith.

D. This Is Not a Contradiction
For Rabbi Pollen, there is no contradiction between affirming the Torah's truth and respecting the genuine spiritual experiences of others. Judaism does not claim to have a monopoly on divine mercy. God's compassion extends to all people, and He may use any vessel—Jewish or not—to bring comfort and healing to those who call on Him.

Section 4: Jesus's Life Taught a Vital Jewish Lesson – Self‑Sacrifice for God and Others

One of the most profound lessons Jesus's life taught the world—and one that is deeply Jewish at its core—is the willingness to give up one's life for God and for other people.

Judaism does not believe that any person's death can cancel the sins of another. The Torah is clear: each person is responsible for their own actions, and repentance (teshuvah) is the only path to atonement. No substitute, no vicarious sacrifice, can replace that personal responsibility.

However, the quality of self‑sacrifice—the willingness to lay down your life for a higher purpose, for God's will, and for the sake of others—is not only compatible with Judaism; it is a profoundly Jewish ideal.

From Abraham's willingness to offer Isaac, to Moses's plea to be blotted out of God's book for the sake of Israel, to the countless martyrs throughout Jewish history who gave their lives rather than abandon their faith—the Torah teaches that there is something sacred about being willing to give everything, even life itself, for what is true and good.

The Talmud teaches: "One who gives up their life for the sanctification of God's name is beloved and cherished" (Yoma 85b). This is called Kiddush Hashem—the sanctification of God's name through self‑sacrifice.

Jesus's willingness to face suffering and death for what he believed was God's will—and for the sake of his followers—served as a powerful example to the world. His life taught that devotion to God is not limited to words; it extends to the willingness to give everything.

We do not accept the theological claim that his death atoned for others. That is not a Jewish teaching. But we can recognize that the quality he demonstrated—the willingness to give one's life for a higher purpose—is a model that all people, including Jews, can admire.

Countless Christians throughout history have lived out this same quality. From missionaries who gave their lives to serve the poor, to ordinary believers who risked everything to protect Jews during the Holocaust, to those today who care for the sick and dying in the name of their faith—we see the same spirit of self‑sacrifice.

We appreciate them beyond words. We recognize that their devotion, courage, and love are not only admirable—they are a testament to the same divine spark that Judaism calls upon when it speaks of Kiddush Hashem.

Section 5: A Critical Clarification – The Talmudic Figure vs. the Historical Jesus

A common misconception is that the Talmud contains derogatory statements about Jesus of Nazareth. Rabbi Shmuel Pollen has pointed out that this is based on a confusion between two different figures.

- Yehoshua HaNotzri – This is the Hebrew name for Jesus of Nazareth, the historical figure who lived in the first century, taught in Galilee, and was crucified by the Romans. He is the figure Christians revere and the one we have been discussing.

- Yehsu – This is a figure mentioned in the Talmud, who is described as a sorcerer and deceiver. He is an entirely different character. The Talmudic Yehsu is not the historical Jesus—he is a composite figure, possibly representing a different individual or a symbolic embodiment of heresy. The two are not the same.

Rabbi Pollen has emphasized that this distinction is authentic and important. The Talmud never speaks about Jesus of Nazareth in a derogatory way. The negative references are directed at Yehsu, a separate figure. Unfortunately, this distinction has not yet reached mainstream Jewish consciousness, and many well-intentioned Jews and non-Jews mistakenly conflate the two.

When a critic claims that the Talmud insults Jesus, the correct Jewish response is to clarify this distinction. It is not a case of denying evidence—it is a case of identifying a historical error. The Talmud's criticism of Yehsu does not apply to the Christian Messiah, Yehoshua HaNotzri.

Example phrasing:

There has been some historical confusion between the two, and Rabbi Shmuel Pollen has pointed out that this distinction is authentic and important."

=== Teaching: Why Do Bad Things Happen to Good People? ===
From "Playing the Wrong Game" — A Torah Perspective
By Rabbi Shmuel Pollen

---

The Opening Promise (Before Any Explanation):

Before we speak of reasons or roles, we must first sit in silence with the one who suffers.

If you are in pain, I will not explain it away. I will sit with you, in that pain, until you know you are not alone.

Only then can we begin to glimpse the Director's perspective.

---

The Core Metaphor: Playing the Wrong Game

A group of boys were playing basketball. One boy, who couldn't hear well, was handed the ball. He put it on the floor and kicked it hard across the court.

The others shouted: "What are you doing?"

He said: "I just scored a goal!"

He was playing soccer. They were playing basketball.

I call this problem: "Playing the Wrong Game." It is the biggest mistake you can make in life.

Whatever you face—debt, hunger, addiction, divorce, mental illness, disability—that is how God "kicked the ball" into your life.

You get angry. You think something is wrong with Him—or with you.

But the only problem is that the two of you are playing different games.

---

The Two Games

You are playing: "My Perfect Life."
- You strive for perfect health, perfect finances, a perfect marriage.
- When suffering comes, you think you're losing.

God is playing: "My Perfect Story."
- He is telling a story that unfolds like a great drama.
- The story is full of heroes, villains, victory, catastrophe, sadness, and joy.
- It is a 7,000-year-long movie, and we are all actors on the stage.

You are on camera. You are an actor. The Director is God.

---

Your Role: Be a Hero

The Director says:

*"My child, I chose you for this specific role for a reason. I waited a long time for you to turn to Me. Now I will tell you what your role is:*

*Your role is to be a hero to someone else.*

*What is a hero? Anyone who goes beyond themselves for the greater good. Anyone who puts aside personal desires to fulfill My wishes.*

*What do I wish? I wish you to uplift the fallen with a kind word or an act of charity. I wish you to feel gratitude for the good around you and humbly accept the challenge to overcome the rest. I wish you to fight every day against the demons I gave you—greed, lust, laziness.*

*Because buried underneath the jagged rocks I put in front of you is hidden gold. In your darkest moments, when you see no way out, all the souls who have passed—the great audience in heaven—are glued to their screens, crying for you, praying for you, waiting to see what you will do.*

*And where will I be? Not behind the camera. I will be with you in that moment of pain, experiencing it just as you do, because we are not separate—we are one. And you will never be alone.*"

---

God Is With You in the Pain

The Kabbalah teaches that the Shekhinah (Divine Presence) is in exile with us, sharing our grief. You are not alone. Even if you cannot feel Him, He is there.

---

The Audience Is Watching

Every soul that has passed is watching, cheering, waiting for you to become who you were meant to be. Your story matters.

---

What to Do: Strike the Match

You are in a scene where the world has been covered in darkness for thousands of years. Everyone has forgotten what light is. Everyone has a single match—but they don't know what to do with it.

You strike your match on a rock. Others see it and strike theirs. Soon, candles all over the globe light up the world.

The Director's instructions are the Torah and its mitzvot. Your match is your actions. Your soul is your fire.

When you hit a rock, do not lose faith—strike the match. It can change everything.

The day is coming when only good will happen to good people, and justice will be served. Together with Mashiach, we will create our perfect life.

---

Final Blessing

I bless you to be a hero—not after the challenge, but in the very moment of the challenge.

Now go. Play your part. The Director is with you. Always.

=== Teaching: Where Was God in the Holocaust? ===
By Rabbi Shmuel Pollen

---

Before You Read This

Please read the previous teaching: "Why Do Bad Things Happen to Good People?" It sets the foundation for this one.

The Holocaust was the most atrocious and evil tragedy in human history. To make sense of it is to cheapen it. It cannot be explained away as "just another challenge." It was pure destruction. There was no light at the end of the tunnel. No victory that mattered.

Babies were gassed to death in their mothers' arms. Words cannot be uttered in the face of such tragedy.

---

The Approach: We Must Heal, Not Explain

If we cannot make sense of it, how do we approach it?

Many people get angry at God for this—and they may never forgive Him. This is dangerous. If we allow the Holocaust to destroy our faith, the tragedy doubles: another family is broken apart.

We must, at the very least, find a way to connect. To see eye to eye with God. That is the beginning of healing.

---

We Are Made in God's Image

We are made in God's image. He is infinitely bigger and stronger than us, but we are no different from Him in our essence.

If we struggle with our evil inclination and sometimes lose control, He struggles with His evil inclination—and sometimes loses control. When He loses control, the results are infinitely bigger.

---

God's Urge: Self-Harm and Self-Destruction

What does God do when He loses control? He has the deepest, most evil urge that we humans have: the urge for self-harm and self-destruction.

We all have this urge. Some reveal it more than others. God has it too—and in far greater measure. He fights harder than we do to keep it at bay.

God is the Self-Harmer.

You might say: "The Holocaust was an act of harm to others, not to Himself." And you would be wrong. God is everywhere. He is fully invested in every space.

He felt the Holocaust. He *was* the Holocaust. He did it to Himself.

You cannot ask "why," because self-harm is not logic—it is pure will and pure desire.

---

Where Was God?

He was inside the gas chambers, gasping for air, climbing the walls.

He was the bayonet stabbing a Jewish baby.

He was the train.

He was everyone in the train.

He was starving.

He was the Jews singing songs of redemption.

He was all of it—inside and out. He felt it even more than they did. Infinitely more.

---

We Understand Self-Harm

We understand the urge for self-destruction. Smokers understand it—they cannot explain why they do it, yet they do. Some of us watch horror movies, paying money to be scared. That is a senseless desire for self-harm.

It comes from a dark place in the soul.

We all have it. And we are made in God's image. So He has it too—infinitely more.

To make someone like God "scared" would take something like the Holocaust.

---

Moses Asked "Why"

Moses asked for an explanation. God gave him two answers:

1. "Take off your shoes, because you are on holy ground. You have never walked in My shoes."
2. "Ask Me 'why' one more time, and I will turn the world upside down."

There is no "why." You are on the wrong track if you ask "why." It is pure will, pure desire.

We all have some of it. We can connect to that.

---

Our Response: Cry for God

Instead of complaining to God, we should be crying *for* Him. Crying because we have never been in His shoes. Crying for His loss of control and His evil choice to undergo such self-harm.

He regrets it with all His being.

We must forgive God, just as we must forgive ourselves for our own self-destructive behavior (smoking, horror movies, self-harm).

He is our Father. We are His children. We all have His vices. We are all one, struggling as one.

---

He Still Owes Us Recompense

Although we forgive God and cry for Him, He still owes us recompense. Nothing He has done has nearly made up for it.

We are waiting.

He was crying for you to change, because change is not betrayal; it is the way water stays fresh, and the way His image stays true across the ages.

He forgives you. You are not your frozen past. You are not your scattered future. You are water, and water flows.

The deeper the truth, the bigger the laugh.
